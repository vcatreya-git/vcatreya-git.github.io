Peak-Hour Decision Support System (DSS) for Dark Store Managers

Product Requirements Document

Date: February 2026 | Author: Team Hashiras (Minions)


Note: This PRD assumed that the PM is working to design the Product at Blinkit


Section 1: Executive Summary

The Problem

During peak hours (6-10 PM), Blinkit's dark stores experience operational chaos not because of capacity constraints, but because of an information architecture failure. The Warehouse Management System (WMS) already contains every signal needed to prevent SLA breaches. The problem is that this information does not reach the store manager during the 3-5 minute window when corrective action is still possible.


Managers currently operate with a 4-minute blind spot. By the time they diagnose an issue through physical floor walks (5-15 minutes), the recovery window has closed. They react instead of anticipating. They handle the loudest problem, not the most urgent one.

The Product

PeakPulse is a real-time triage co-pilot for dark store managers. It is not a dashboard. It is a single pane of glass that delivers prioritized, prescriptive action cards to wherever the manager physically is, with enough lead time to act before orders breach SLA.


The system has four core modules: 

  • Operations Command Center providing all KPIs in one view with real-time SLA gauge, order summary, and hourly trend visualization; 
  • Predictive Alert System delivering forward-looking warnings with recommended actions and 30-minute demand lookahead; 
  • Smart Picker Assignment routing orders based on picker proficiency (Expert/Mid/New classification); and 
  • Inventory Discrepancy Tracker flagging physical-digital mismatches with severity scoring before they cascade.

Section 2: Market Context & Strategic Rationale

(Aided by primary and secondary research performed using Google, Claude + Industry experts)

India Quick Commerce Scale

The Indian quick commerce market has grown to $5+ billion (2025 projected), with 1,500-2,000 industry dark stores. Blinkit operates 1,816 stores across 100+ cities with a target of 3,000 stores by March 2027. Customer expectations have compressed to 10-15 minute delivery windows. A high-demand dark store processes 1,200-2,000 orders per day with SKU counts ranging between 3,000-5,000 items per store.

Peak-Hour Economics

The evening window (6-10 PM) is where quick commerce economics are won or lost. Order volume surges 30-50% above baseline within 20-30 minutes, with a typical store doing 600 orders/day concentrating 40% of volume in this 4-hour window. Weather events (rain) and cultural moments (IPL matches) can spike demand 2-4x within 30 minutes. Even a 5% SLA drop during peak impacts hundreds of orders daily across the network.


When dark stores struggle during peak hours, the cascading effects include direct cost spikes from refunds and compensation payouts, customer trust erosion through dropped ratings and decreased repeat usage, manager attrition from chronic firefighting, and deteriorating unit economics despite volume gains. At 1,800+ stores, even a 5% improvement in peak-hour SLA translates to tens of thousands of recovered orders per day and hundreds of crores in annual margin recovery.

Strategic Timing

HSBC research confirms that leading quick commerce players are now expected to moderate expansion and focus on optimizing existing operations. The playbook is shifting from "open more stores" to "extract more value from each store." Blinkit's integration with Zomato's ecosystem provides a structural moat through cross-category consumption signals (food ordering patterns predict grocery demand), shared logistics learning, and data density that enables more accurate ML models for demand prediction.



Section 3: Problem Statement & Scope Boundaries

(Aided by primary and secondary research performed using Google, Claude + Industry experts)

Problem Statement

During peak hours, Blinkit's high-demand dark stores experience operational chaos due to decision overload and limited real-time clarity for managers. 

The core failure: The right information does not reach the right person at the right time in a usable form.


The WMS already contains every signal needed to prevent most SLA breaches: real-time order queue depth, per-picker throughput rates, inventory discrepancy logs, and rider availability and ETA data. None of this information reaches the manager in a prioritized, actionable format during the 3-5 minute window when intervention would prevent breach.

What This Product IS and IS NOT

IS: PeakPulse is a real-time decision support system for dark store managers, a triage layer that ranks problems by recoverability (not loudness), a prescriptive tool that recommends actions (not just displays data), and an ambient system that reaches managers wherever they are physically located.


IS NOT: It is NOT a supply chain redesign, a demand forecasting tool, a rider management system (that is the fleet coach's domain), a replacement for manager judgment, or a physical infrastructure change. We are not expanding store size or adding equipment beyond existing devices.

Section 5: Peak-Hour Workflow & Decision Gap Analysis

(Aided by primary research of multiple dark stores - Details in the APPENDIX)

Current State: The Three Phases of Dark Store Operations


When Instability Begins: The 4-Minute Gap

Core Decision Bottlenecks

Diagnosis Gap: A floor walk takes 5-15 minutes. The recovery window is 3-5 minutes. The manager is structurally guaranteed to arrive too late for any issue discovered through physical observation.


Task Conflict: The paper checklist (pre-shift tasks) and live crisis (peak-hour signals) compete for attention. No system reprioritizes for the manager.

Invisible Performance Variance: A new joiner taking 6 minutes per order is indistinguishable from a system-wide slowdown on the aggregate dashboard. The right intervention requires per-picker data that exists but is never surfaced.


Loudness Bias: Under 4+ simultaneous signals, the human brain handles whoever is most vocal, not whoever is most urgent. The manager handles the cluster manager call, the rider at the counter, the packer shouting. The manager misses the silent Zone B batch breaching SLA in 4 minutes. Estimated cost: 15-20 orders breach SLA unnoticed per peak session, approximately Rs 1.9 lakh/month in preventable SLA failures per store.

PeakPulse is a real-time triage co-pilot that delivers prioritized, actionable insights to wherever the manager physically is, with enough lead time to act before orders breach SLA. It transforms the manager from a firefighter reacting to the loudest crisis into a commander with situational awareness of the entire battlefield.

Feature 1: Operations Command Center (P0)


What it does: Single view showing all operational KPIs against targets, enabling the manager to assess store health in under 10 seconds.


Key metrics displayed: 

  • SLA Gauge (color-coded: Green >85%, Yellow 80–85%, Red <80%)
  • Hourly SLA Trend sparkline
  • Orders Summary (Fulfilled / On-Time / Delayed / Cancelled)
  • Demand vs Capacity Gauge
  • Peak Hour Indicator (Normal / Elevated / Peak / Surge)
  • Rider Summary with 30-minute availability prediction
  • Picker Summary with idle detection
  • Inventory Health by severity
  • Store vs cluster comparison


User interface: 

  • Color-coded status (Green / Amber / Red)
  • Progressive disclosure (summary default, tap to drill down)
  • Shift-aware adaptation
  • Auto-refresh every 30 seconds
  • Dark theme UI optimized for low-light store environments


Delivery: Desktop (primary) + Tablet-optimized (10") + Mobile responsive. No new hardware required.


Success criteria: Manager can assess store health in under 10 seconds; context-switching between systems reduced by 80%.

Section 10: Moonshot Features

Primary Moonshot: Drone-Assisted Rider Intercept

The Problem: During peak hours, riders are the primary bottleneck. A rider finishes a drop-off 4 minutes away from their next pickup, but an urgent order is sitting at a store with its SLA clock ticking.

The Concept: Smart Intercept catches this scenario in real-time. A drone flies directly to the rider (not to the customer). The rider's bike has a small locked docking cradle mounted on the rear rack. The drone lands precisely on the cradle, the box locks shut automatically, the rider enters OTP to release the package into a sealed compartment, the drone detaches and returns autonomously. This eliminates the "rider return trip" bottleneck entirely. The entire handoff takes under 60 seconds; riders never leave their bike.

Prototype Status: A Drone Dispatch Overview panel has been included in the PeakPulse prototype to demonstrate the future-ready architecture.

Section 11: UX Principles & Information Architecture

Design Principles for Peak-Hour Stress

  • Glanceable: Every signal interpreted in < 3 seconds. No reading text during peak.
  • Progressive Disclosure: Start with minimum (what to do). Allow drill-down (why) only if tapped. Most managers will never tap.
  • Color-Coded Priority: Red = Act now (breach imminent); Amber = Monitor (trending negative); Green = Healthy (on track).
  • No Confrontation Design: Performance data shows position numbers (Picker 3, Picker 7), not names. Prevents interpersonal conflict while enabling routing decisions.
  • Shift-Aware Interface: Pre-peak mode (planning-focused), Peak mode (action-focused, only critical metrics), Post-peak mode (review-focused, RCA and handover tools).
  • One-Tap Actions, Auto-Refresh (30 sec), Dark Theme for low-light environments.

Section 13: MVP Scope & Rollout Plan

Phase 1: Pilot (Week 1-2)

Deploy Operations Command Center and basic Predictive Alert System (volume surge, SLA risk alerts only) at 3 high-volume stores in Bangalore (Koramangala, HSR, Indiranagar). Desktop and tablet-optimized, mobile-responsive.

Store selection criteria: Orders per day >800, existing manager tenure >3 months, reliable connectivity, willingness to participate (opt-in).

Success gates: Manager can use dashboard without training; zero critical system failures; initial qualitative feedback positive.

Team: 2 backend engineers, 1 frontend engineer, 1 data engineer, 0.5 PM, 0.5 designer.

Phase 2: Validation (Week 3-4)

Collect usage data and manager feedback. Measure SLA impact metrics vs control stores. Identify top friction points.

Success gates: >50% daily active usage among pilot managers; measurable reduction in decision latency; clear signal on SLA improvement (directional).

Phase 3: Iteration (Week 5-6)

Address top 5 feedback items from pilot managers. Fix critical bugs. Deploy Smart Picker Assignment and Inventory Discrepancy Tracker.

Success gates: Feedback items resolved; >70% adoption; picker routing accuracy >85%.

Phase 4: Expansion (Week 7-8)

Roll out to 20 stores across Bangalore. Mobile-native experience refinement.

Success gates: >5% SLA improvement vs control; consistent metrics across 20 stores; positive manager NPS.

Phase 5: Scale (Month 3+)

Expand to Delhi, Mumbai (100+ stores). ML model refinement. Integration with fleet management. Cluster manager portal for multi-store view.

Success gates: Consistent metrics at scale; no degradation; 15% reduction in peak-hour SLA breaches.

Phase 6+: Moonshots (Month 5+)

Voice assistant pilot (5 stores), drone intercept feasibility study, cross-ecosystem integrations.

Known Limitations (MVP)

The Lovable prototype uses simulated data; production deployment requires integration with Blinkit's order management, rider management, and inventory systems. Alert rules are initially hardcoded; production version should support configurable thresholds per store. "30-minute lookahead" in MVP is based on simple heuristics; ML-based prediction requires a data pipeline not yet built. MVP is designed for single store view; multi-store (Area Manager view) requires data model expansion. Dashboard requires active internet connection; offline capability planned for future phase.


Section 15: Appendices

Appendix A: Primary Research Summary

Source 1 (Rahul's Interview - Demand/Supply Operations): Picker productivity at 5 to 6 units per man minute; expected order is 4 orders / minutes; rider calculation at 2.5 orders per hour with 100 riders needed per shift and 12 at store at any instant; core failure area is "manager does not have visibility of which SKU mix will come in orders"; planogram drift occurs "during receiving/putaway."


Source 2 (Praditi's Store Visit - DLF Phase 5): 3-shift operation (6AM-3PM, 3PM-11PM, 10PM-7AM); OD worker model via Blinkit onboarding app; manager described system as "well-oiled machine" but "gives RCA to SM" for delays; audit process is core to store operations.


Source 3 (Customer Review Analysis): Top negative themes by volume: Wrong/Missing/Substituted Items (25-30%), Poor Quality/Expired Items (15-20%), Late Delivery/Order Not Delivered (5-10%).

Appendix B: Key Calculations

Order Volume Context: Typical dark store handles 600-800 orders/day; high-demand stores handle 1,200-2,000 orders/day. Calculations use high-volume benchmarks (2,500 orders/shift) to stress-test capacity requirements.


Rider Requirements: Orders per shift: 2,500; orders per rider per shift: 25 (9 hours x 2.5 orders/hour, accounting for breaks); riders needed per shift: 100; orders per minute at peak: 4; riders needed at store at any instant: 12 (4 x 3); average return ETA: ~12 minutes.


SLA Impact Math: Preventable breaches per session: 15-20 orders; AOV: Rs 520; daily preventable loss: Rs 7,800-10,400; monthly per store: Rs 1.9-2.5 lakh; network (1,800 stores): Rs 34-45 crore/month potential recovery.
