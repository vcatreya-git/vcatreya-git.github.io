YouTube CaseStudy

Problem Statement:

YouTube’s long-form content discovery and session-start flow do not reliably support sustained viewing. Users frequently fail to discover long-form videos that match their demonstrated interests, and when relevant content is surfaced, the transition from discovery to playback introduces friction that prevents users from committing to a long-form session. This is a platform-level product deficiency, not a content supply or audience demand issue, and it impacts users across both free and Premium tiers.

This initiative aims to fix YouTube’s long-form discovery and engagement system by ensuring users can reliably find long-form content that matches their demonstrated interests and move from discovery into sustained viewing sessions with minimal friction.

Why This Problem Matters:

Long-form video commands significantly higher CPM (Cost Per Mille) than Shorts, as advertisers pay premium rates for mid-roll placements in 10–30 minute videos where viewers are more engaged and attentive. A platform where users consume more Shorts and fewer long-form videos therefore earns less ad revenue per user per hour. YouTube’s TV strategy positioning the platform as a living-room destination to compete with Netflix and other streaming services depends directly on growing long-form watch time on large screens. At the creator level, the YouTube Partner Program is still primarily driven by long-form watch time; while Shorts monetization exists, it pays only a fraction of long-form rates. If creators cannot sustain long-form production financially, YouTube’s content depth will erode and with it, the platform’s primary competitive differentiation from pure short-form alternatives.

Discovery Objectives

Primary Objectives

Objective 1 — Restore Long-Form Visibility in Discovery

YouTube’s aggressive push into Shorts has systematically displaced long-form content on the home feed, even for users with a demonstrated history of long-form viewing. The objective is not to reverse YouTube’s Shorts strategy, but to protect and restore long-form discoverability within that constraint.

Targets:

  • Increase long-form content (>10 minutes) share in personalized feeds by 40% during high-availability sessions
  • Reduce time-to-relevant-discovery from 15+ minutes to under 5 minutes

Objective 2 — Reduce Friction Between Discovery and Session Start

Finding relevant long-form content and starting a sustained viewing session are two distinct steps, and the platform consistently fails at the second. Watch Later exists but functions as a passive list with no intelligence: the average user has 20+ saved videos and completes fewer than 12% of them. Every UI interruption between discovery and playback—overlays, subscribe prompts, autoplay countdowns—represents a leak in the funnel.

Targets:

  • Make Watch Later and Continue Watching accessible in under 2 clicks from any discovery surface
  • Introduce a Distraction-Free Viewing Mode that suppresses non-critical UI interruptions for a user-configured session length (1–2 hours)

Objective 3 — Shift Recommendations from Click Optimization to Match Quality

The recommendation engine currently optimizes for clicks, not match quality. For long-form content—where users are committing 20–90 minutes—this distinction is critical. Approximately 35% of long-form sessions end within 8 minutes, indicating the content was directionally relevant but wrong in depth or quality. The system registers this as partial success and reinforces the same imprecise recommendations.

Targets:

  • Improve long-form recommendation accuracy by 60%, measured as clicks resulting in ≥50% video completion
  • Reduce irrelevant recommendations by 45%, measured as clicks abandoned within 2 minutes

Market & Industry Research

YouTube sits at an inflection point. Long-form content still commands the dominant share of total viewing time, yet the algorithm, ad infrastructure, and homepage surface area are increasingly oriented around Shorts. This research synthesizes primary source data from YouTube’s 2026 CEO letter, third-party industry reporting, and platform analytics to map the forces shaping long-form engagement, identify the structural gaps driving drop-off, and validate the product opportunities outlined in this PRD.

Three structural tensions define the current environment:

  • Long-form drives disproportionate viewing time, but short-form dominates discovery and homepage real estate.
  • AI tooling is democratizing content creation at scale, simultaneously enabling creativity while flooding the platform with low-quality material that degrades viewer trust.
  • Ad load has reached a breaking point, driving Premium growth while suppressing session depth and satisfaction for the majority of ad-supported users.

Key Industry Signals

1. Long-Form Is the Platform’s True Revenue Engine — Treat It That Way
 73% of U.S. viewing time is long-form, yet homepage surface area, recommendation momentum, and new feature investment are heavily skewed toward Shorts. Product investment must restore long-form discovery pathways, particularly on the homepage and within the Shorts-to-long funnel.

2. AI Is a Double-Edged Sword — The Slop Crisis Is Real and Measurable
 Low-effort AI content is already receiving a 5.44× traffic penalty, signaling a measurable trust problem. Platform trust is at stake. Product solutions must differentiate authentic, AI-assisted human content from fully automated slop through mechanisms such as verified badges, scoring engines, and transparency labeling.

3. Ad Fatigue Is Suppressing Long-Form Sessions and Driving Behavior Change
 Shorts consumption grew 25% as ad-fatigued viewers seek relief in short clips, while session length on the free tier declined 8% in Q1 2025. Improving the ad experience—via micro-payments, Premium expansion, or smarter ad placement—directly unlocks long-form session depth.

4. 74% of Shorts Views Are Non-Subscribers — An Untapped Conversion Opportunity
 The top-of-funnel audience is massive and largely unconverted. A seamless Shorts-to-long-form pathway (e.g., swipe-to-continue, channel surfacing, auto-subscribe flows) represents a meaningful opportunity to convert this audience into long-form viewers.

5. Premium Users Are the Ideal Long-Form Audience — Build for Them
 Premium users watch 38 minutes per session on average, increase watch time by 28% post-upgrade, and are 2.4× more interactive. Optimizing for Premium viewer behavior—bingeability, quality, and depth—aligns creator incentives with the platform’s highest-value and most loyal users.

6. AI Navigation Tools Have Proven Demand at Scale
 20 million users used the “Ask” AI feature in December 2025 alone, validating strong demand for intelligent in-video navigation. This directly supports investment in Smart Chapters, AI co-pilots, and intent-based playlist curation.

7. The vidIQ 2026 “100,000 Channel” Study: The Retention Split Is Widening
 Analysis of over 100,000 channels shows a significant retention dip for videos in the 3–7 minute range. Users are bifurcating their behavior: they use Shorts for discovery and quick answers, or they commit to 20+ minute “masterclasses” for deep learning.
 This creates a “Hollow” zone: content that is too long for quick answers but too short for deep-dive authority fails to trigger the algorithm’s loyalty signals.

8. The Biggest Device Shift in YouTube’s History Has Already Happened
 TV surpassed mobile as YouTube’s #1 viewing device in the U.S. in early 2025 for the first time. This is not an incremental shift—it is a platform identity change. YouTube can no longer be described as mobile-first. Mobile is now primarily top-of-funnel discovery, while TV has become the primary surface for long-form depth and completion.

The numbers are striking. YouTube now commands 12.7% of all U.S. TV viewing time, making it The numbers are striking. YouTube now commands 12.7% of all U.S. TV viewing time, making it the single largest streaming destination. Over 1 billion hours of content are watched on TVs every day, and U.S. adults are projected to spend three more minutes per day watching YouTube than watching Netflix in 2026. This is not a marginal shift—it represents a fundamental change in how YouTube is consumed.

Mobile, however, is not declining—it is serving a different role. Mobile viewers primarily consume content on-the-go: while commuting, waiting in line, or during short breaks. Shorts and vertical videos dominate this context because they fit naturally into the screen orientation and usage patterns. The implication is a clear funnel split: mobile functions as the top-of-funnel for discovery (Shorts and lightweight exploration), while TV has become the depth-of-funnel surface for long-form viewing and session completion.

User Segmentation

Segments are defined by behavior, not demographics. Each represents a distinct long-form discovery failure mode.

Segment 1 — The Committed Viewer (~20% of MAU)
Arrives with specific intent and knows the topic or creator they want. Fails because recommendation precision is poor: “Up Next” suggests content that is directionally correct but wrong in depth or quality.

Segment 2 — The Opportunistic Watcher (~32% of MAU)
Open to long-form but arrives without specific intent. Fails because the home feed defaults to Shorts and trending content regardless of session context. Leaves without starting a long-form session, not due to lack of interest but due to lack of relevant surfacing.

Segment 3 — The Intent-Blocked User (~28% of MAU)
Finds content they want but cannot commit to at the moment (commuting, at work, time-constrained). Fails because Watch Later is a passive list with no intelligence, no prioritization, and no proactive re-surfacing.

Segment 4 — The Drifted User (~18% of MAU)
Was once an active long-form viewer, but the platform has lost an accurate model of their current interests due to algorithmic drift and stale signals. Recommendations no longer reflect who they are.

User Behavior Analysis

1. The Discovery–Abandonment Loop
 Users open the home feed, find nothing compelling within 30–60 seconds, and exit or pivot to Shorts. Median scroll depth before abandonment is 4–6 cards—meaning long-form content is often never even seen. Root cause: long-form content is systematically ranked below position 6 on the home feed, where scroll abandonment is already high.

2. The Wrong-Fit Click
 Approximately 35% of long-form sessions end within the first 8 minutes. Users click content that is directionally relevant but imprecise in depth or quality, disengage early, and the system treats this as partial success—reinforcing similar but still imprecise recommendations. 22% of these users immediately re-enter search, confirming the intent was real but the match failed.

3. Watch Later as Dead Storage
 Watch Later additions are the strongest demonstrated intent signal available: a user explicitly flagging content they want to watch. Yet completion rates are below 12%. Videos watched within 72 hours of saving complete at ~58%; after 7 days, that drops to 19%. The signal exists. The product does nothing with it.

4. Session Context Is Ignored
 Long-form completion rates vary dramatically by context: ~15% on mobile weekday mornings, ~44% on TV weekday evenings, and ~52% on TV weekend afternoons. The platform serves the same feed regardless. Adapting discovery surfaces to device and time-of-day context is a high-leverage, tractable fix.

5. Two Distinct Discovery Modes
 Users split into two behavioral groups that require different recommendation logic:

  • Creator-anchored users (~40%) discover through trusted channels and subscriptions. They need a creator similarity graph based on depth and quality, not just topic overlap.
  • Topic-anchored users (~60%) discover through search and cross-creator browsing. They need sub-topic depth classification so recommendations match their level of knowledge, not just their area of interest.

Ads Are a Major Pain Point

  • 74% of users report that ads do bother them; only 26% agree that “ads don’t bother me at all.”
  • 68% say they pay for Premium specifically to escape ads (with 35% strongly agreeing), making ad avoidance the single strongest motivation for Premium subscriptions.
  • 55% report that mid-video ads cause them to abandon long videos before finishing.

Long-Form Content Fatigue Is Real (But Split)

  • 50% of users feel that sitting through a 10+ minute video is “too much effort,” indicating a divided audience: attention fatigue is growing, but not yet universal.
  • Combined with the ad abandonment data above, long-form videos face a dual pressure: ad friction and content fatigue.

Shorts-to-Long Discovery Is Broken

  • 68% of users say YouTube rarely connects their Shorts viewing to long videos they would enjoy. This is the highest agreement rate in the entire survey, indicating that the recommendation system is failing to bridge short-form discovery into long-form consumption.

AI Summaries Are Quietly Reducing Watch Time

  • 50% of users say that when YouTube summarizes a video, they no longer feel the need to watch it.
  • 46% prefer reading an AI summary over watching the full video. While a slight majority (54%) still prefer watching, nearly half are now open to skipping the video entirely.
  • This indicates that AI features, while high-utility, may be cannibalizing actual video views.

Bottom Line

Users are frustrated by ads (74%), increasingly fatigued by long content (50%), and underserved by a discovery system that fails to funnel Shorts viewers into long-form videos (68%). At the same time, AI summaries—while not yet dominant—are already convincing roughly half of users that they may not need to watch the full video, posing a material risk to long-form engagement.

User Pain Points

The market and device shift described above (mobile as discovery, TV as depth) has created a clear pattern: users increasingly discover content easily, but struggle to commit to long-form viewing and complete sessions. Our survey data, market analysis, and competitor research point to four structural pain points that explain why long-form engagement is breaking down—despite strong underlying demand.

These are not isolated UX issues. Each represents a system-level failure in how discovery, ranking, monetization, and session experience currently work together.

1. The “Zero-Click” Crisis (When Useful Content Is Misread as Uninteresting)

What’s happening (in simple terms):
 YouTube now offers high-utility AI tools like “Ask” and summaries that let users get answers without watching the full video. This is good for users—but the system still measures success mainly through watch-time metrics like APV (Average Percentage Viewed). As a result, videos that are genuinely helpful can look “unsuccessful” to the algorithm simply because users got what they needed quickly.

User Pain Point:
 Users get the answer, but miss nuance and depth. Creators, meanwhile, see their reach drop even when their content is highly useful, which reduces the incentive to produce deep, high-effort long-form videos.

Why this matters:

  • The platform is misreading helpfulness as low interest.
  • This slowly suppresses high-quality educational and explanatory content.

Evidence:

  • 20 million users used “Ask” in December 2025 to bypass timeline scrubbing.

2. AI Content Poisoning (When Discovery Feels Lower Quality Over Time)

What’s happening (in simple terms):
AI tools have made it extremely easy to produce content at scale. In the Shorts feed especially, the system still optimizes heavily for “Swipe-Through Rate” (how fast people move past a video). This rewards high-volume, low-effort content and makes it harder for thoughtful, human-made content to stand out.

User Pain Point:
Users experience “discovery fatigue.” The feed starts to feel repetitive, shallow, or low-value, and the platform feels like it is losing its “soul.” This leads to shorter sessions and more abandonment, especially for long-form viewing.

Why this matters:

  • Even if long-form still commands most viewing time, its visibility and perceived quality are being diluted.
  • Over time, this erodes user trust and brand perception.

Evidence:

  • AI “slop” is scaling at ~10× the rate of human content.
  • Long-form still accounts for ~73% of U.S. viewing time but is losing discovery surface area.

3. The Discovery Gap (When Shorts Don’t Lead to Depth)

What’s happening (in simple terms):
 Users increasingly discover topics through Shorts, but the product does a poor job of helping them continue into deeper, long-form content. Shorts and long-form effectively behave like two separate systems, with little support for moving from “interest sparked” to “session started.”

User Pain Point:
The experience feels fragmented. After watching a Short on a topic they like, users have to manually search or hunt for deeper content instead of being guided there naturally.

Why this matters:

  • Shorts are a massive top-of-funnel, but that interest is not being converted into long-form engagement.
  • This leaves a large growth opportunity untapped for both users and creators.

Evidence:

  • 74% of Shorts views come from non-subscribers.
  • The home feed’s long-form discovery surface dropped from 6 videos per row to 2 in late 2025.

4. Session Fragility (When Ads Break the Viewing Experience)

What’s happening (in simple terms):
 Long-form videos require sustained attention, but the current ad load for free users (5–7 mid-roll ads per video) repeatedly interrupts the viewing experience. This makes long-form content feel “expensive” in terms of attention and effort.

User Pain Point:
 Users experience interruption exhaustion. The flow state needed for 10+ minute videos keeps getting broken, leading to early exits and unfinished sessions.

Why this matters:

  • The product is structurally pushing users away from long-form, even when the content itself is good.
  • This creates a growing gap between free and Premium users.

Evidence:

  • Premium (ad-free) users have 3.5× higher session completion rates than free users.

In summary

Across these four pain points, a consistent pattern emerges:
Users want depth, but the current discovery, metrics, and session experience make long-form hard to start, hard to trust, and hard to finish. Fixing long-form engagement therefore requires addressing not just one UI issue, but the full chain from discovery → evaluation → session → completion.

Creator Ecosystem Insights

The discovery gap is not only a viewer problem. It is a supply-side crisis in slow motion. The structural failure that prevents users from finding long-form content is simultaneously degrading the economics and sustainability of the creators who produce it.

  • The revenue cliff between formats is unsustainable. A creator earning $5,000 monthly from long-form might pull $200–$400 from Shorts with equivalent views. Long-form RPMs average $1–$25+ per 1,000 views. Shorts RPMs sit at $0.01–$0.30 in most markets. Creators also keep only 45% of Shorts ad revenue versus 55% on long-form — a structurally inverted split baked into the monetisation architecture. In Q3 2025, Shorts achieved per-watch-hour revenue parity for YouTube as a platform. Sundar Pichai confirmed on the October 2025 earnings call that "in the U.S., Shorts now earn more revenue per watch hour than traditional in-stream." This is good for Alphabet. It raises an uncomfortable question: if Shorts generate equivalent platform revenue at a fraction of the production cost, what is the platform's long-term incentive to fix long-form discovery at all?
  • The creator who builds the bridge bears 100% of the cost. 70% of channels now post Shorts, yet long-form engagement has not risen proportionally — because without a platform-level conversion mechanism, creator-level Shorts investment produces diminishing returns. Channels that manually build the bridge through end screens and pinned comments see 41% faster growth. The majority who cannot execute this are leaving the opportunity entirely unmeasured. Meanwhile, subscribers gained through Shorts have 60–80% lower engagement than those gained through long-form — meaning a Shorts-built audience is structurally different from a long-form audience, and no platform tool currently bridges the two. 
  • The best long-form creators are already de-risking. Nebula — a creator-owned platform built explicitly because creators felt the YouTube algorithm penalised depth and experimentation — now has 680,000 paid subscribers and 175 creators representing over 120 million YouTube subscribers. Spotter, which financed long-form creators by licensing their catalogues, laid off 40% of its staff after missing financial goals directly attributed to Shorts pulling views from long-form. When the investment infrastructure around long-form starts contracting, the signal has moved beyond individual creator decisions. 
  • Burnout is accelerating — and broken economics are the primary driver. A July 2025 survey of 1,000 creators found 52% have experienced burnout and 37% have considered quitting entirely. When ranked by severity, financial instability was the number one cause — cited by 55% of those burned out. For long-form creators specifically, financial instability traces directly to the discovery problem: no discovery means no watch time, no watch time means no Partner Program revenue, and no revenue means no sustainable production. Billion Dollar Boy / Censuswide, July 2025. Via Viral Nation. 

The current dynamic is a self-reinforcing loop: broken discovery suppresses long-form watch time → declining revenue pushes creators toward cheaper Shorts → increased Shorts volume degrades the interest signals any discovery bridge would rely on → and the platform's own revenue incentives now reduce the internal urgency to solve it. The window to build the bridge is open. It is not permanently open.

Solution Direction Overview 

The primary goal of this roadmap is to transition YouTube from a Passive Feed (where the platform guesses what users want) to an Active Intent Engine (where the platform helps users achieve what they are trying to do).

This overview serves as the North Star for the 2026 product roadmap, shifting YouTube from a “Time-on-App” model to an “Intent-and-Integrity” model.

Strategic Pillars

Pillar 1: Intent Bridge (The Gesture Funnel)
 We collapse the “Discovery Gap” by implementing a “Swipe to Continue” / “Swipe Right” feature at the end of Shorts to watch the corresponding long-form video. This carries session context directly into the full video, eliminates the ~16-minute “search tax,” and makes the move from a Short (teaser) to a Long-form video (full story) a native gesture rather than a search task.

Pillar 2: Metric Pivot (The Task Success Score)
 We solve the Zero-Click Crisis by reclassifying “Ask” and “Summarize” events as high-value engagement. Creators are rewarded for information density and problem-solving rather than just raw watch time. This reduces reliance on Average Percentage Viewed (APV) as the sole indicator of quality.

Pillar 3: Trust Filter (Proof of Human)
 To combat AI poisoning, we introduce a “Verified Human” badge for channels with less than 5% AI-generated content. This allows users to actively filter low-effort content out of their discovery feed.

Pillar 4: Session Lubricant (The Deep Watch Incentive)
 We stabilize session fragility for free users by awarding 30 minutes of ad-free viewing for every 60 minutes of long-form completion. This reduces “structural exit” points and also serves as a Premium conversion funnel.

MoonShot Idea : 

Make YouTube long-form videos interactive, similar to Scarlet Hollow—where viewers make choices that change the story or outcome of the video.

Introduce native branching video technology into YouTube’s creator toolkit, enabling creators to build interactive long-form experiences where viewers can make choices that alter the story, outcome, or learning path of a video. This transforms passive watching into active participation, directly addressing the core engagement problem: passive long-form viewing is losing ground to active short-form scrolling.

Netflix has already validated this concept with Bandersnatch (Black Mirror, 2018) and You vs. Wild, where real-time viewer choices changed story outcomes and drove significantly higher engagement than passive content. YouTube’s structural advantage is that creators, not the platform, bear most of the production cost, making this format viable at platform scale in a way it never was for Netflix.

By building native branching video tools directly into Creator Studio, YouTube would enable creators across gaming, education, fitness, and storytelling to create interactive experiences without relying on third-party tools. This directly targets the core problem: a viewer who is actively making choices cannot simultaneously scroll Shorts, helping rebuild the attention commitment that passive long-form viewing has lost to short-form consumption.

The primary risk is metric reliability. Views and Average Percentage Viewed break down when every viewer takes a different content path, which means YouTube would need to define new engagement metrics before this format can be fairly evaluated and monetized.

High-Level Solution Flow

This flow integrates the “Task Success” score directly into the viewer’s journey to ensure creators are credited even when a user relies on AI summarization. It also illustrates how the system bridges the gap from a 60-second Short to a 20-minute sustained viewing session.

Step-by-Step Logic

1. Intent Initiation (Pillar 1)
 The user finishes a Short that acts as a “trailer.” A “Swipe to Continue” prompt appears, instantly loading the long-form video with zero search latency.

2. Integrity Check (Pillar 3)
 The discovery engine prioritizes the long-format video because it carries a “Verified Human” badge, ensuring the user is not diverted to low-quality or AI-generated slop.

3. Active Engagement (Pillar 2 – The Zero-Click Solution)
 The user opens the “Ask” feature to get a summary or ask a specific question about the video.

4. The System Change
 Instead of counting this action as an “exit,” the system logs a “Task Success” event.

5. Creator Credit
 The creator receives engagement credit and a portion of the ad-revenue share for providing the information that satisfied the user’s intent.

6. The Deep Dive Incentive (Pillar 4)
 Prompted by the AI summary, the user chooses to watch a specific “Smart Chapter” of the video. After 10 minutes of sustained viewing, they receive a notification: “Deep Watch Reward: Next 30 mins are Ad-Free.”

7. Data Loop
 The recommendation engine uses the specific question asked in Step 3 to refine the user’s homepage with similar high-value long-form content, further reducing future search fatigue.

Impact on Users & Creators

User Impact

These changes focus on making discovery faster, viewing smoother, and content more trustworthy by directly addressing user pain points such as fatigue, friction, and interruptions.

  • Faster Discovery: Reduce the “search tax” (time-to-relevant-discovery) from an average of over 15 minutes to under 5 minutes.
  • Reduced Interruption: The “Session Lubricant” (ad-free reward) mitigates “interruption exhaustion” caused by current ad density (5–7 mid-roll ads) by rewarding 60 minutes of long-form viewing with 30 minutes of guaranteed ad-free time.
  • Seamless Viewing: Introduce simple mechanisms (under 2-click access) to reduce “session fragmentation.” Currently, 78% of users do not resume long-form content when switching devices.
  • Higher Utility: Encourage AI summary tools (such as “Ask”) so users can resolve their intent faster without missing nuance or needing to scrub the timeline.
  • Trust in Content: The “Trust Filter” addresses “discovery fatigue” and “AI slop” by allowing users to filter content that does not carry the “Verified Human” badge.

Creator Impact

The strategy focuses on aligning monetization with real user value and converting high-volume Shorts traffic into higher-value long-form audiences.

  • Financial Alignment: The “Zero-Click” Crisis is addressed by reclassifying “Ask/Summarize” events as high-value engagement, rewarding creators for problem-solving rather than penalizing them for low Average Percentage Viewed (APV).
  • Efficient Audience Funnel: The “Intent Bridge” (Shorts-to-Long funnel) is designed to capture the 74% of non-subscriber Shorts views and convert them into the long-form ecosystem.
  • Protected Revenue: The “Trust Filter” helps protect the platform’s $30B advertiser moat by prioritizing verified, human-centric content. Creators with less than 5% AI-generated content receive a “Verified Human” badge.
  • Improved Retention: By reducing session friction for free users, the platform aims to close the completion gap; Premium users currently have 3.5× higher session completion rates than free users.
  • Sustained LTV: The overall strategy preserves the financial sustainability of long-form production, which continues to command significantly higher CPM than Shorts monetization.

Risks & Trade-offs


Finalized Risks & Trade-off

Strategic Pillar

Primary Risk (The Downside)

Core Trade-offs (What We Give Up for Long-Term Gain)

Pillar 1: Intent Bridge (Shorts-to-Long Funnel)

User Frustration: The "Swipe to Continue" feature might make Shorts feel like advertisements, potentially annoying viewers and causing lower Shorts engagement.

Sacrificing Short-Term Speed: We are prioritizing building a clear pathway from Shorts to long videos, even if it slightly slows down the high-speed, entertaining Shorts feed.

Pillar 2: Metric Pivot (Task Success Score)

Metric Gaming: Rewarding creators for "Task Success" (like using the "Ask" summary feature) might encourage them to create content designed only to be summarized, not watched fully.

Trading Raw Watch Time for Value: We are moving away from the simple, measurable metric of Average Percentage Viewed (APV). This shift to rewarding User Intent could cause short-term confusion for advertisers.

Pillar 3: Trust Filter (Proof of Human)

Creator Alienation: Defining and enforcing the <5% AI-generated content rule is technically difficult, risking false positives that could penalize legitimate human creators.

Trading Scale for Integrity: We are intentionally suppressing the massive volume of low-effort AI content ("slop") to prioritize the platform's Brand Trust and content quality.

Pillar 4: Session Lubricant (Ad-Free Incentive)

Revenue Loss & Premium Devaluation: Giving free users temporary ad-free viewing reduces short-term ad revenue and may make existing paid subscribers feel their $13.99 Premium subscription is less valuable.

Trading Near-Term Revenue for Long-Term Loyalty: We are accepting an immediate loss in ad revenue to reduce interruptions for free users, with the goal of driving deeper viewing sessions and Premium customer conversion.

System Risk: AI/Bot Over-Reliance

Algorithmic Collapse: If new AI features (like the Task Success Score or Verified Human badge) are quickly gamed by sophisticated bots, the entire discovery engine could fail by promoting fraudulent, non-human signals.

Trading Simplicity for Optimization: This shift requires massive, constant investment in "cat-and-mouse" bot-detection technology and complex anti-gaming infrastructure.

Future Scope & Scalability

1) Extend the Intent Bridge and Task Success loop across all discovery surfaces
 The Shorts-to-Long “Swipe to Continue” funnel and the “Task Success” scoring loop should expand beyond Shorts and Home into Search, Subscriptions, Watch Later, and TV surfaces. Any place where a user expresses intent should be able to route them into a sustained long-form session.
 Discovery failure is systemic, not limited to one surface: search fatigue, dead Watch Later lists, and homepage displacement all contribute to abandonment. Scaling these mechanisms across surfaces compounds their impact, reduces the 15+ minute “search tax,” and turns intent signals (saves, questions, partial views) into a platform-wide engine for long-form engagement.

2) Gradually deepen the quality of intent and integrity signals used by the ranking system
 Today’s signals start with “Ask/Summarize” (Task Success) and the “Verified Human” badge. Over time, this can expand to include chapter-level navigation, rewatch behavior, save-and-return patterns, and session completion context to refine match quality for long-form recommendations.
 Click-based optimization produces wrong-fit sessions (~35% drop within 8 minutes) and reinforces imprecise recommendations. As more high-signal behaviors are incorporated, the system can move further away from raw APV toward true match quality and intent satisfaction.

3) Evolve creator incentives and formats around depth, structure, and sustained sessions
 With Task Success, Smart Chapters, and the Deep Watch Incentive in place, the platform can progressively reward creators who build structured, high-density long-form content and experiment with deeper formats (e.g., interactive or chapter-driven narratives).
 Broken discovery is also a supply-side crisis: creators are drifting toward cheaper Shorts because long-form economics are fragile. By scaling incentives that favor intent resolution, structure, and completion, YouTube can stabilize long-form production, improve content quality, and protect the platform’s long-term differentiation versus short-form competitors.

BEHAVIORAL & COGNITIVE HYPOTHESES

B1

Dopamine Recalibration

Continuous exposure to the variable-ratio reinforcement schedule of Shorts (like a slot machine) raises the reward threshold, making the linear pacing of long-form content feel like "work" rather than leisure. The brain craves rapid dopamine hits and finds slower, deeper content unrewarding at the point of initiation.

Moderately Supported — neuroscience is real but correlation ≠ causation. TV long-form is growing, which suggests the effect is context-dependent, not permanent.

B2

Attention Span Decline

Average human attention spans are measurably decreasing (reportedly ~8 seconds), making users unwilling or unable to engage with content over 10 minutes. The first 15 seconds of any video is now the make-or-break point for retention.

Screen-based attention: 150s (2004) → 47s (present) per Dr. Gloria Mark, UC Irvine. 77% of YouTube views in 2025 are Shorts. However, University of Plymouth & Open University (2025–26) confirm humans are still capable of 2-hour cinema experiences.

Social Currency / FOMO

Users consume Shorts as a social prerequisite to stay in the loop with peer discussions, prioritising breadth of knowledge (Shorts) over depth (long-form). FOMO acts as the primary acquisition trigger for Shorts.

69% of millennials experience FOMO regularly. 56% worried about missing important social updates. 8 in 10 teenagers say social media makes them feel connected. Large creators see 29 new subs per 10K Shorts views vs 11 per 10K long-form views.

Partially Supported — FOMO phenomenon is real and documented, but "Shorts as social prerequisite" is an inference, not a direct finding. The creator sub-rate gap is correlation, not causation.

B4

Search Fatigue / Paradox of Choice

The cognitive cost of selecting a long-form video (average 11–16 minute "search tax" per session) has exceeded the perceived reward. Shorts eliminate the burden of choice entirely — users prefer zero-latency gratification over the decision paralysis of browsing a long-form catalogue.

Americans spend 110 hrs/year (16 min/session) just deciding what to watch. 49% would cancel a service if content is too hard to find. Nielsen: only 1/3 of adult streaming users bother browsing; 21% give up watching entirely. Global search time averages 14 minutes per session.

Partial Support — the search tax is real and measurable. However, the hypothesis conflates platform UX failure (poor discovery) with a user cognitive limitation. The fix may be product, not behaviour.

PRODUCT & PLATFORM HYPOTHESES

P1

The Discovery Gap (Main Hypothesis)

The Shorts and long-form recommendation systems are entirely siloed. Topic interest demonstrated via Shorts behaviour is never surfaced to the long-form recommendation engine for any user — free or Premium. The platform fails to convert 74% of non-subscriber Shorts views into any long-form engagement, leaving a measurable conversion opportunity entirely unmanaged.

74% of Shorts views from non-subscribers. Home feed dropped from 6 long-form videos per row to 2 in late 2025 (-67%). Shorts use swipe-through rate; long-form uses avg view duration — systems share no data. Creators who manually bridge (end screens, CTAs) see 41% faster channel growth. 12–18 new subs per 10K Shorts views (negligibly small conversion rate).

Strong — the strongest and most actionable hypothesis in the document. Directly measurable, product-solvable, and supported by multiple independent data points. The open question is why YouTube has not built the bridge yet.

P2

Zero Click Crisis (Metric Failure)

New AI features like YouTube's native "Ask" allow users to summarise long videos in seconds, generating zero views and crushing the Average Percentage Viewed (APV) metric. This incorrectly signals to the algorithm that the content is uninteresting, suppressing high-value educational content from recommendations — even when the content successfully resolved user intent.

20M users used the "Ask" feature in December 2025 alone (YouTube CEO Neal Mohan, 2026 Annual Letter). Education is the most frequently watched genre: 500M+ educational videos viewed daily. AI summarisation projected to reduce viewing time by 25% by end of 2026. The metric system is at war with the user utility being built.

Strong — points to a genuine systemic metric failure. If the platform measures success by Time on Platform while building tools that Save Time, the North Star is internally contradicted.

P3

High Friction / Session Fragility

For free users, mid-roll ad density (5–7 per video by 2025) acts as a structural exit prompt, making long-form content costly to sustain. The "Cost of Attention" exceeds the "Value of Content" at each ad break, triggering session abandonment. Premium users, as a natural control group, prove the engagement capacity exists — it is only the friction variable that differs.

Mid-roll completion rates fell from 82% to 57% when ad length exceeded 20 seconds. Premium users watch 32% longer per session and complete long-form videos at 3.5x the rate of free users. 47% of users now use ad blockers (up from 27% in 2020). 36% of Premium conversions driven by desire to escape non-skippable ads.

Medium-Strong — perfect description of user sentiment but risky as a product policy basis. Reducing ads risks Creator Exodus as RPMs decline. Self-selection caveat: Premium users may be inherently more intentional viewers.

P4

AI Content Poisoning (Brand Equity Erosion)

AI-generated "slop" scales in the Shorts feed at 10x the rate of human content. The algorithm promotes it because it achieves strong swipe-through metrics, but it generates no reliable signal of genuine user interest, degrading the discovery foundation. Users experience "Discovery Fatigue" — the platform is losing its "soul" — and high-value advertisers will exit an ecosystem perceived as low-quality brainrot.

21% of new-user Shorts feeds are AI-generated slop; 33% are low-value brainrot (Kapwing study, Oct 2025). 42%+ of creators now use AI tools to generate or edit Shorts. Low-effort AI content already receiving 5.44x traffic penalty. Top AI slop channels generate $4–4.25M/yr in ad revenue, creating strong financial incentive to continue.

Strong for brand equity risk. Even if platform metrics appear healthy (users still swiping), the "Soul of YouTube" is being eroded. Loss of the $30B advertiser moat is a credible tail risk.

P5

Recommendation Metric Misalignment

YouTube's ranking system optimises for clicks and raw watch time rather than match quality or task completion. For long-form content — where users commit 20–90 minutes — this distinction is critical. ~35% of long-form sessions end within 8 minutes because the content is directionally relevant but wrong in depth or quality. The system registers this as a partial success and reinforces the same imprecise recommendations.

35% of long-form sessions end within 8 minutes. 22% of those users immediately re-enter search, confirming intent was real but match failed. Watch Later completion rate: <12% overall; 58% if watched within 72hrs, dropping to 19% after 7 days. Long-form mobile completion ~15% on weekday mornings vs ~52% on TV weekend afternoons — yet platform serves the same feed regardless.

Strong supporting evidence. The Watch Later "dead storage" signal is particularly compelling — it is the strongest demonstrated intent signal on the platform and is completely ignored.

P6

Monetisation Model Misalignment (Creator Side)

YouTube's monetisation model for long-form video is a one-size-fits-all approach that fails to account for heterogeneous content types (learning, tutorials, podcasts, storytelling). Combined with the Shorts revenue cliff ($0.01–$0.30 RPM vs $1–$25+ for long-form), creators are structurally incentivised to produce Shorts over long-form, slowly depleting the long-form content supply.

Creator earning $5K/month from long-form might earn $200–$400 from Shorts with same views. Creators keep 45% of Shorts ad revenue vs 55% for long-form. Shorts achieved per-watch-hour revenue parity for YouTube platform (Sundar Pichai, Q3 2025 earnings). Spotter laid off 40% of staff due to Shorts cannibalising long-form views.

Strong supply-side argument. Burnout and financial instability are cited by 55% of creators as the primary burnout driver. If economics don't support long-form production, content supply erodes before the discovery fix arrives.
